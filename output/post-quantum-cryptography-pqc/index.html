Post-quantum cryptography (PQC)

Cryptographic algorithms designed to be secure against the potential threats posed by quantum computers.

Description

Post-quantum cryptography (PQC) refers to cryptographic algorithms and protocols that are believed to be secure against the powerful computational capabilities of quantum computers. Traditional cryptographic systems, such as RSA and ECC, rely on the difficulty of certain mathematical problems (like factoring large integers or solving discrete logarithms) that can be efficiently solved by quantum algorithms, particularly Shor's algorithm. As quantum computing technology advances, there is a pressing need to develop cryptographic methods that remain secure even in a post-quantum world. PQC aims to accomplish this by using mathematical problems that are currently considered hard for both classical and quantum computers, such as lattice-based problems, hash-based signatures, multivariate polynomial problems, and code-based cryptography. The development of PQC is critical for ensuring the integrity and confidentiality of data in various applications, including secure communications, data storage, and digital signatures, in an era where quantum computers could potentially undermine existing security protocols.

Examples

  • Lattice-based cryptography (e.g., NTRU)
  • Hash-based signatures (e.g., XMSS)

Additional Information

  • The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) is leading efforts to standardize PQC algorithms.
  • PQC is crucial for future-proofing security systems against emerging quantum threats.
