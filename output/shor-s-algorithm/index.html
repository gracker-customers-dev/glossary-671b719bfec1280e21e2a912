Shor’s algorithm

A quantum algorithm for integer factorization that runs exponentially faster than the best-known classical algorithms.

Description

Shor's algorithm, developed by mathematician Peter Shor in 1994, revolutionizes the field of cryptography by demonstrating that quantum computers can efficiently factor large integers. This capability poses a significant threat to widely used cryptographic systems, such as RSA, which rely on the difficulty of factorization for security. While classical algorithms, such as the general number field sieve, require exponential time to factor large numbers, Shor's algorithm can accomplish this in polynomial time, specifically O((log N)^2 (log log N) (log log log N)), where N is the integer to be factored. The algorithm leverages quantum mechanics, making use of phenomena such as superposition and entanglement to perform calculations that are infeasible for classical computers. As quantum computing technology progresses, the implications of Shor's algorithm become increasingly critical, necessitating the development of post-quantum cryptographic algorithms that can withstand potential quantum attacks.

Examples

  • Shor's algorithm can factor the number 15 into its prime factors 3 and 5 in polynomial time.
  • If a quantum computer were to run Shor's algorithm on a 2048-bit RSA key, it could potentially break the encryption in a matter of hours.

Additional Information

  • Shor's algorithm is a foundational example of quantum computing's potential impact on cybersecurity.
  • Research is ongoing in developing quantum-resistant algorithms to secure data against future quantum attacks.
